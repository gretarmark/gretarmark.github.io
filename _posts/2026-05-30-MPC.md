𝐌𝐮𝐥𝐭𝐢𝐯𝐚𝐫𝐢𝐚𝐛𝐥𝐞 𝐌𝐏𝐂 𝐨𝐧 𝐚𝐧 𝐈𝐧𝐝𝐮𝐬𝐭𝐫𝐢𝐚𝐥 𝐁𝐞𝐧𝐜𝐡𝐦𝐚𝐫𝐤 (𝐅𝐨𝐮𝐫-𝐒𝐭𝐚𝐠𝐞 𝐄𝐯𝐚𝐩𝐨𝐫𝐚𝐭𝐨𝐫)

Model Predictive Control is one of the most impactful advanced control strategies in industry. By explicitly handling multivariable interactions and constraints, MPC can increase throughput, improve product quality, and reduce energy consumption, all simultaneously.

I implemented a full closed loop MPC simulation on a classical industrial benchmark: a four-stage evaporator system (KU Leuven DaISy dataset) used to reduce the water content of a product, for example milk. All signals are normalized and mean-centered, and the dataset does not include physical units.

𝐒𝐲𝐬𝐭𝐞𝐦 𝐨𝐯𝐞𝐫𝐯𝐢𝐞𝐰:

• 𝐈𝐧𝐩𝐮𝐭𝐬: feed flow, vapor flow, cooling water flow
• 𝐎𝐮𝐭𝐩𝐮𝐭𝐬: dry matter content, product flow, product temperature
• 𝐌𝐨𝐝𝐞𝐥: state-space model identified from process data using N4SID subspace identification

𝐖𝐡𝐚𝐭 𝐭𝐡𝐞 𝐫𝐞𝐬𝐮𝐥𝐭𝐬 𝐬𝐡𝐨𝐰:

Dry matter content tracks its setpoint tightly, as intended, since product quality was given the highest priority in the cost function.

Product flow and temperature show small steady-state offsets, which is the natural consequence of a prioritized, multivariable cost function. Adjusting the output weights shifts the trade-off between quality, throughput, and temperature control.

The Kalman filter estimates track the noisy measurements closely throughout, which is a good sign that the identified model captures the process dynamics well.

The controller is currently unconstrained, which leads to aggressive input actions during setpoint changes. The next step is adding input and rate of change constraints, which is where MPC really earns its place over classical controllers.
